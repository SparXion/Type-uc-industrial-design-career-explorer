# UCID | App Manifestations

**2D Representations**
These methods focus on flat, visual depictions to convey ideas quickly, often as the initial stage in design ideation, requiring skills in sketching, composition, and visual communication.
* Hand-drawn sketches (pencil, ink, markers on paper)
* Digital sketches (using tablets like Wacom or software like SketchBook)
* Orthographic projections (top, front, side views)
* Perspective drawings (one-point, two-point, three-point)
* Exploded views (showing assembly disassembly)
* Wireframe diagrams (basic outlines without shading)
* Storyboards (sequence of sketches for user journeys)
* Mood boards (collages of images, colors, textures)
* Concept illustrations (detailed artistic renderings)
* Technical drawings (dimensioned blueprints with annotations)
* Schematic diagrams (flowcharts of functions or processes)
* Isometric drawings (3D-like on 2D plane)
* Axonometric projections (parallel lines for depth)
* Sectional views (cutaways showing internals)
* Elevation drawings (flat views of facades or sides)
* Plan views (top-down layouts)
* Rendered 2D images (photorealistic via Photoshop or Illustrator)
* Vector graphics (scalable line art in Adobe Illustrator)
* Digital paintings (using brushes in Procreate or Corel Painter)
* Thumbnail sketches (small, rapid idea explorations)
* Gesture drawings (quick captures of form and motion)
* Line art (clean outlines without fills)
* Shaded renderings (adding gradients for volume)
* Color studies (exploring palettes and materials)
* Texture maps (2D representations of surface finishes)
* Icon designs (simplified symbolic representations)
* Infographics (data visualizations of design specs)
* Annotations and callouts (labels on drawings)
* Hand-lettered typography (integrated text in sketches)
* Collage techniques (mixing photos and drawings)

**3D Digital Representations**
Digital tools allow for precise manipulation and iteration of concepts in virtual space, demanding abilities in software proficiency, spatial reasoning, and rendering techniques.
* 3D modeling (using software like Rhino, SolidWorks, Fusion 360)
* Parametric modeling (variable-driven designs in Grasshopper or Inventor)
* Surface modeling (organic shapes in Alias or Blender)
* Solid modeling (volumetric forms in CATIA or Creo)
* Polygonal modeling (mesh-based in Maya or 3ds Max)
* NURBS modeling (smooth curves in Rhino)
* SubD modeling (subdivision surfaces for high-res)
* BIM modeling (Building Information Modeling for architectural products)
* CAD drawings (Computer-Aided Design files)
* Digital sculpting (ZBrush or Mudbox for detailed forms)
* Photorealistic renderings (KeyShot, V-Ray for lifelike images)
* Real-time renderings (Unity or Unreal Engine previews)
* 3D animations (keyframed motion in Cinema 4D)
* Simulations (physics-based in ANSYS or COMSOL)
* Finite Element Analysis visualizations (stress/strain maps)
* Assembly animations (showing part interactions)
* Exploded 3D views (digital disassembly)
* Turntable renders (360-degree rotations)
* Augmented reality previews (AR apps like Adobe Aero)
* Virtual prototypes (interactive 3D models in VR headsets)
* Holographic projections (Microsoft HoloLens representations)
* Procedural generation (algorithmic designs in Houdini)
* Topology optimization models (lightweight structures)
* Generative design outputs (AI-assisted variations)
* UV mapping and texturing (applying materials digitally)
* Lighting setups (virtual studio lighting for renders)
* Camera path animations (fly-throughs of designs)
* Mesh optimizations (low-poly for efficiency)
* Boolean operations models (union, subtract for forms)
* Spline-based modeling (curves for precise contours)

**Physical Models and Prototypes**
Tangible manifestations enable hands-on testing of form, function, and ergonomics, highlighting skills in craftsmanship, material knowledge, and iterative building.
* Hand-sculpted models (using clay, foam, or wood)
* Clay sculptures (traditional pottery wheel or freeform)
* Foam core models (quick cuts with hot wire or knives)
* Cardboard mockups (low-fidelity prototypes from recyclables)
* Kitbashed models (assembling from existing parts or toys)
* 3D printed models (FDM, SLA, SLS techniques)
* CNC machined prototypes (milled from wood, plastic, metal)
* Laser-cut assemblies (precision parts from acrylic or plywood)
* Vacuum-formed shells (plastic sheets over molds)
* Injection molded and assembled (small runs with molds)
* Cast resin models (poured into silicone molds)
* Metal fabricated prototypes (welded or bent sheets)
* Woodworked models (lathe-turned or carved)
* Paper prototypes (folded origami-like structures)
* Wireframe physical models (bent wire for skeletons)
* Scale models (miniature versions for proportion testing)
* Full-scale mockups (life-size for user testing)
* Functional prototypes (works-like with moving parts)
* Aesthetic prototypes (looks-like with finishes)
* Hybrid prototypes (combining digital prints with hand additions)
* Soft goods prototypes (sewn fabrics for wearables)
* Electronics-integrated models (Arduino or Raspberry Pi embeds)
* Biodegradable prototypes (eco-materials like PLA)
* Sand-cast models (for metal testing)
* Blow-molded prototypes (hollow plastics)
* Rotational molded models (for large hollow items)
* Thermoformed prototypes (heated plastics over forms)
* Laminated models (layered materials like plywood)
* Forged metal prototypes (hammered shapes)
* Etched prototypes (chemical or laser for details)
